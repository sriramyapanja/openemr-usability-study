# Clinical Context

## Why EHR Usability Matters

Electronic Health Records (EHRs) are the operational backbone of modern healthcare — the primary interface through which clinical decisions are documented, medications are ordered, diagnoses are coded, and patient histories are shared across care teams. As of 2021, over 96% of hospitals and 78% of office-based physicians in the United States had adopted certified EHR systems (ONC, 2022). Yet adoption has not translated uniformly into improved care outcomes. A substantial and growing body of evidence links poor EHR usability to clinician burnout, documentation errors, and patient safety events.

This context document situates the OpenEMR usability evaluation within the broader clinical informatics landscape — explaining why usability matters, how it connects to measurable outcomes, and how the patterns identified in this study parallel challenges in large commercial EHR environments.

## The Usability Problem in EHRs

### Cognitive Load and Error Risk

Healthcare documentation is cognitively demanding by nature. Clinicians must simultaneously manage patient interaction, clinical reasoning, and real-time data entry — tasks that draw on overlapping cognitive resources. EHR interfaces that impose **extraneous cognitive load** — load arising from poor design rather than task complexity — compound this burden and increase the probability of error (Patel et al., 2021).

Common sources of extraneous load in EHR systems include:
- Excessive click paths for high-frequency actions
- Non-intuitive navigation that misaligns with clinical workflow sequence
- Undifferentiated alert stacking that dilutes attention
- Interface states that do not reflect clinical mental models (e.g., requiring encounter creation before vitals entry)

All four of these patterns were observed in the current evaluation of OpenEMR.

### Clinician Burnout and Documentation Burden

Physician burnout — with rates exceeding 50% in several specialties — has been directly linked to EHR usability. A landmark study by Shanafelt et al. (2016) found that EHR time and documentation burden were among the strongest predictors of professional dissatisfaction. Subsequent research confirmed that physicians in the United States spend approximately two hours on EHR documentation for every one hour of direct patient care (Sinsky et al., 2016).

Usability interventions — simplifying navigation, reducing click paths, supporting efficient documentation — are not merely quality-of-life improvements. They are a direct mechanism for reducing burnout and protecting the clinical workforce.

### Patient Safety Implications

The clinical consequences of poor usability extend directly to patients. A Joint Commission Sentinel Event Alert (2015) identified EHR design as a contributing factor in a range of adverse events, including:
- Medication errors arising from interface confusion
- Incorrect patient identification during documentation
- Alert fatigue leading to overrides of clinically significant warnings
- Delayed or missed diagnoses due to documentation workflow disruptions

The most critical finding of the present study — that diagnosis and medication entries cannot be corrected once made — maps directly onto this safety concern. A system that prevents error correction is a system that allows errors to propagate.

## OpenEMR in Context

### What Is OpenEMR?

OpenEMR is the world's most widely installed open-source EHR and practice management platform, certified by the Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (ONC) and used in over 100 countries (OpenEMR, 2024). Its open-source nature makes it uniquely accessible for research, education, and deployment in resource-limited settings — and uniquely transparent as a subject of usability evaluation. Unlike commercial systems, OpenEMR's codebase can be directly modified in response to the findings of studies like this one.

### Parallels to Commercial EHR Ecosystems

While OpenEMR is an open-source system, the usability patterns identified here are not unique to it. Many of the same issues — alert fatigue, complex navigation, misaligned workflow sequencing, limited record correction — have been documented in commercial EHR systems including Epic, Cerner, and Meditech (Ratwani et al., 2018; Kroth et al., 2019). This means that the recommendations generated by this study are not only applicable to OpenEMR but generalizable to EHR usability improvement efforts more broadly.

**Parallel to Epic:** Epic's problem list module supports an "Enter in Error" correction pathway — a feature whose absence in OpenEMR was the highest-scoring usability gap in this study (RC_001, RC_002, difficulty 9/9). This gap represents a direct, implementable improvement for OpenEMR that would bring it to parity with commercial system best practices.

**Alert fatigue in Epic and Cerner** has been extensively documented (Ancker et al., 2017; Nanji et al., 2014), and tiered alert systems have been proposed and piloted in both environments — making R-04 of this study's recommendations actionable with reference to an established evidence base.

## Health Informatics as a Career Context

### Why This Matters for the Next Generation of Health Informaticists

The clinical informatics profession sits at the intersection of healthcare, data science, and human-computer interaction. Graduates entering this field will work alongside clinicians, engineers, and administrators to shape how health information systems are designed, implemented, evaluated, and improved.

Understanding EHR usability — not just as an abstract design concept but as a measurable, testable, clinically consequential property — is foundational to this work. The skills demonstrated in this evaluation:
- Structured task design and systematic scoring
- Workflow gap identification and heuristic mapping
- Translating usability findings into actionable, prioritized recommendations

...are directly applicable to roles in EHR optimization, clinical informatics consulting, health IT product management, and clinical data engineering.

### Workforce Landscape

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 17% growth in health information management and informatics roles through 2030 — well above the average for all occupations. Organizations including health systems, EHR vendors (Epic, Cerner/Oracle Health, athenahealth), consulting firms (Deloitte, Accenture), and federal agencies (ONC, CDC, CMS) actively recruit candidates who combine clinical domain knowledge with informatics and analytical skills.

The competencies demonstrated in this project — EHR evaluation, clinical data analysis, workflow documentation, and evidence-based recommendation development — directly align with the qualification profiles sought by these employers.
